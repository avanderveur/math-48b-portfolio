Rational Functions Overview

Rational functions are fractions of polynomials. Their graphs can have breaks, asymptotes, and holes depending on the numerator/denominator.

Definition

A rational function is any function that can be written as:

f(x) = P(x) / Q(x)
where P(x) and Q(x) are polynomials and Q(x) ≠ 0.

Because the denominator cannot be zero, rational functions have restricted domains and special graph behavior.

Key Features

1

Domain restrictions: exclude denominator zeros.

2

Vertical asymptotes: denominator zeros that don't cancel.

3

Holes: canceled factors create removable discontinuities.

4

Horizontal/slant asymptotes: end behavior from degree.

5

x-intercepts: simplified numerator = 0.

6

y-intercept: evaluate f(0) if defined.

Summary

Rational functions are polynomial fractions with restricted domains. Their graphs are shaped by intercepts, asymptotes, and holes. A full analysis always starts with factoring and simplifying.
